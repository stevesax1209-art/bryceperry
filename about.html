My Story

The Journey Behind the Mission

I was 40 years old when the tremors started. Not dramatic, not debilitating ‚Äî just a slight, persistent shake in my left hand that I kept dismissing as stress, overwork, too much coffee. After months of tests, specialist appointments, and an MRI, I sat in a neurologist's office and received three words that rewrote everything: Parkinson's disease. I walked out with a pamphlet and a prescription, and absolutely no idea what to do next.

The pamphlet showed images of elderly patients. The online forums were dark, filled with fear and grief. I was active, building a career and a family ‚Äî and I couldn't find a single person who looked remotely like me. What I needed didn't exist. So I did what made sense at the time: I started building it myself. A small online group became a podcast. The podcast became a community. The community became Doing Life Today ‚Äî a platform that now reaches over 100,000 people worldwide ‚Äî built in just one year.

Along the way, I learned things no textbook teaches. I learned that the most powerful thing you can offer someone in crisis isn't medical advice ‚Äî it's your honest story. I learned that community isn't a nice-to-have in chronic illness; it's clinical. It changes outcomes. I learned that technology, thoughtfully built with patients at the center, can dramatically improve daily life ‚Äî which led me to found the Parkinson's Community Network (PCN) App, a mobile tool for medication tracking, symptom logging, and AI-assisted support. I learned that my voice, once I stopped being afraid to use it, could open doors I never imagined.

Today I speak on stages across North America ‚Äî at medical conferences, patient advocacy summits, corporate wellness events, and community gatherings. Every talk I give is rooted in the same conviction that got me through my darkest days: your diagnosis is not your destination. The people who show up in that audience aren't looking for pity or platitudes. They're looking for proof that life on the other side of a hard thing can still be full, meaningful, and even joyful. I get to be that proof. It is the greatest privilege of my life.
